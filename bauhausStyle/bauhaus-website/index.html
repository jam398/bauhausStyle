00 — BACKGROUND

The Bauhaus Legacy

Founded in 1919 in Weimar, Germany, the Bauhaus school revolutionized design by uniting art, craft, and technology.

The school's philosophy—that form should follow function—continues to influence contemporary design, from architecture to digital interfaces. Neo-modernism takes these principles and applies them to today's digital landscape.

In the digital age, Bauhaus principles manifest through minimalist interfaces, geometric layouts, systematic color use, and typography that prioritizes clarity and readability. Companies like Linear, Stripe, and Vercel exemplify this approach.

03 — PIONEERS

Designers Who Shaped Bauhaus

Innovators who combined art, craft, and technology—and whose influence still guides modern interface design.

Architect

Walter Gropius

1883–1969

Founder and first director of the Bauhaus. Architect and educator who united art and technology under the principle “form follows function.”

Key Works

Bauhaus Dessau building, Fagus Factory

Photographer

László Moholy-Nagy

1895–1946

Hungarian artist who championed light, typography, and experimental media. Brought Bauhaus thinking to the U.S. via the New Bauhaus in Chicago.

Known For

Photography, typography, experimental film

Typographer

Herbert Bayer

1900–1985

Graphic designer who developed the “Universal” typeface and set the foundation for modern information design and brand systems.

Key Works

Universal typeface, Bauhaus exhibition systems

Color Theorist

Josef Albers

1888–1976

Artist and teacher whose “Homage to the Square” studies proved color is relative—insight that still guides interface palettes and accessibility.

Known For

Interaction of Color, Homage to the Square
