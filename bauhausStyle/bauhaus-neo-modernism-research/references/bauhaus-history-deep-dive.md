## What Was the Bauhaus?

### Official Name
**Staatliches Bauhaus** (State Bauhaus)

### Definition
The Bauhaus was a revolutionary German art school that operated from 1919 to 1933. It combined crafts and fine arts, teaching that design should serve society through functional, affordable, mass-produced objects and architecture.

### Core Mission
"The ultimate goal of all artistic activity is building!" — Walter Gropius, 1919 Manifesto

**Revolutionary Ideas:**
- No distinction between artist and craftsman
- Unity of all creative disciplines
- Design for industrial mass production
- Teaching through making (hands-on experimentation)
- Geometry and abstraction over ornamentation
- Form follows function

### Duration & Impact
- **Active:** 1919-1933 (14 years)
- **Students:** ~1,400 total
- **Faculty:** 30+ master artists/craftsmen
- **Legacy:** Foundation of all modern design education

## Historical Context: Post-WWI Germany

### The Weimar Republic (1919-1933)
**Political Climate:**
- Germany defeated in WWI (1918)
- Kaiser Wilhelm II abdicated
- Weimar Republic established (democratic government)
- Economic instability, hyperinflation
- Social upheaval, political extremism rising
- Desire for reconstruction and renewal

**Cultural Atmosphere:**
- Rejection of pre-war imperial aesthetics
- Embrace of modernism and experimentation
- Neue Sachlichkeit (New Objectivity) movement
- Cabaret culture, expressionist art
- Optimism about technology and progress
- International exchange of ideas

### Arts & Crafts Background
**Precedents:**
- **William Morris & Arts & Crafts Movement** (1860s-1910s, UK)
  - Reaction against industrial shoddiness
  - Quality craftsmanship
  - But elitist, expensive, anti-machine
  
- **Deutscher Werkbund** (1907-1934, Germany)
  - Founded by Hermann Muthesius
  - Integration of art, industry, craftsmanship
  - Standardization for mass production
  - Direct precursor to Bauhaus

- **De Stijl** (1917-1931, Netherlands)
  - Piet Mondrian, Theo van Doesburg
  - Geometric abstraction
  - Primary colors + black/white
  - Influenced Bauhaus aesthetics

### The Building Metaphor
**"Bauhaus" = "Building House" / "House of Building"**
- Architecture as mother of all arts
- All disciplines work toward building
- Construction as metaphor for society-building
- Craft guilds of medieval cathedrals reimagined

## The Three Locations & Eras

### 1. Weimar Era (1919-1925)
**Location:** Weimar, Thuringia, Germany  
**Director:** Walter Gropius

**Characteristics:**
- Expressionist, mystical beginnings
- Influenced by Johannes Itten's spiritual teaching methods
- Handcraft emphasis (weaving, pottery, metalwork)
- Vorkurs (preliminary course) established
- Exhibitions demonstrate progress
- Political opposition from conservative Thuringia government

**Key Manifesto (1919):**
> "Architects, sculptors, painters, we all must return to the crafts! For art is not a 'profession.' There is no essential difference between the artist and the craftsman. The artist is an exalted craftsman."

**Why They Left:**
- Right-wing government hostile to progressive school
- Budget cuts and political pressure
- Gropius sought more supportive location

### 2. Dessau Era (1925-1932)
**Location:** Dessau, Saxony-Anhalt, Germany  
**Directors:** Walter Gropius (1925-1928), Hannes Meyer (1928-1930), Ludwig Mies van der Rohe (1930-1932)

**Characteristics:**
- **Most productive period**
- Purpose-built Bauhaus building designed by Gropius (iconic modernist architecture)
- Shift toward functionalism and industrial design
- "Art and Technology: A New Unity" motto
- Mass production focus
- Student dormitories, integrated living/learning

**Gropius Period (1925-1928):**
- Building completed 1926
- International recognition
- Masters' houses for faculty
- Typography workshop flourishes (Herbert Bayer)

**Hannes Meyer Period (1928-1930):**
- Communist sympathies, social focus
- "The needs of the people instead of the need for luxury"
- Architecture as scientific, social problem-solving
- Wallpaper and furniture designs for mass market
- Political tensions increase

**Mies van der Rohe Period (1930-1932):**
- Depoliticized curriculum
- Architecture and interior design focus
- Elegant, refined modernism
- Attempted to save school from Nazi pressure
- Closed by Dessau city council (Nazi-controlled) in 1932

### 3. Berlin Era (1932-1933)
**Location:** Berlin (private institution)  
**Director:** Ludwig Mies van der Rohe

**Characteristics:**
- Private school in abandoned telephone factory
- Minimal operations, survival mode
- Nazi harassment and intimidation
- Gestapo raid (April 1933)
- Voluntary closure (July 1933) to avoid Nazi control

**End of the Bauhaus:**
- Hitler declared Bauhaus "un-German" and "Bolshevist"
- Faculty and students emigrated (USA, Israel, UK, Switzerland)
- Legacy continued through diaspora

## Key Figures & Their Contributions

### Founders & Directors

#### Walter Gropius (1883-1969)
**Role:** Founder & Director (1919-1928)

**Background:**
- German architect
- Werkbund member
- Designed Fagus Factory (1911) - early modernist industrial building

**Contributions:**
- Founded Bauhaus with visionary manifesto
- Combined fine arts academy with crafts school
- Designed Dessau Bauhaus building (1926) - iconic glass curtain wall
- Established curriculum structure
- Promoted international collaboration

**Philosophy:**
- "Architecture is the ultimate aim of all creative activity"
- Unity of art and technology
- Design for the masses, not luxury

**Post-Bauhaus:**
- Emigrated to UK (1934), then USA (1937)
- Taught at Harvard Graduate School of Design
- Spread Bauhaus pedagogy in America
- TAC (The Architects Collaborative) practice

#### Hannes Meyer (1889-1954)
**Role:** Director (1928-1930)

**Background:**
- Swiss architect
- Socialist/Communist political views
- Social housing advocate

**Contributions:**
- Emphasized social responsibility in design
- Scientific, functionalist approach
- Affordable housing and furniture
- Architecture as collective, social endeavor

**Philosophy:**
- "Building is a biological process, not an aesthetic one"
- Design serves society, especially working class
- Architecture is social science, not art

**Controversy:**
- Political activism disrupted school
- Dismissed by Dessau mayor for communist activities

#### Ludwig Mies van der Rohe (1886-1969)
**Role:** Director (1930-1933)

**Background:**
- German-American architect
- "Less is more" philosophy
- Refined modernist aesthetic

**Contributions:**
- Depoliticized curriculum to protect school
- Focused on architecture and craftsmanship
- Elegant, minimalist design aesthetic
- Attempted negotiation with Nazi authorities

**Philosophy:**
- "God is in the details"
- Refinement and precision
- Honest expression of materials and structure

**Post-Bauhaus:**
- Emigrated to USA (1937)
- Director of Architecture at Illinois Institute of Technology
- Designed Seagram Building, Farnsworth House, Barcelona Pavilion
- Epitome of International Style architecture

## The Bauhaus Curriculum

### Preliminary Course (Vorkurs)
**Duration:** 6 months (mandatory for all students)

**Purpose:**
- Unlearn previous academic training
- Explore materials, forms, colors
- Develop individual creativity
- Prepare for workshop training

**Taught by:**
- Johannes Itten (1919-1923) - spiritual, expressive
- László Moholy-Nagy (1923-1928) - technological, experimental
- Josef Albers (1923-1933) - material economy, color theory

**Key Exercises:**
- Material studies (paper, wood, metal, glass, textile)
- Contrast explorations (light/dark, rough/smooth, etc.)
- Color theory experiments
- Form analysis (geometric shapes)
- Three-dimensional construction
- Drawing from nature (abstraction)

### Workshop Training
**Duration:** 3 years

**Structure:**
- **Two masters per workshop:**
  - **Form Master** (artist) - taught design theory, composition
  - **Technical Master** (craftsman) - taught material techniques

**Required Workshops (students rotated through several):**
1. **Stone** - sculpture
2. **Wood** - carpentry, furniture
3. **Metal** - functional objects, lighting
4. **Clay** - pottery, ceramics
5. **Glass** - stained glass, painting on glass
6. **Weaving** - textiles, tapestries, fabrics
7. **Painting** - murals, wall painting
8. **Printing** - typography, graphic design
9. **Stage** - theater, performance, costume
10. **Architecture** (advanced students only)

### Journeyman Certificate
After 3 years, students could earn journeyman certificate through examination, then continue to master's level or join faculty.

### Teaching Philosophy
- **Learning by doing** (hands-on experimentation)
- **Integration of theory and practice**
- **No copying historical styles**
- **Embrace industrial materials and methods**
- **Form follows function**
- **Art and technology united**

## Core Design Principles

### 1. Form Follows Function
**Definition:** Design should be determined by its purpose, not decoration.

**Application:**
- No applied ornamentation
- Honest expression of materials
- Structure visible and logical
- Every element serves a purpose

**Example:**
- Breuer's Wassily Chair (1925) - tubular steel structure exposed, no decorative elements, lightweight, stackable

### 2. Unity of Art and Technology
**Definition:** Embrace machine production as artistic opportunity, not enemy.

**Breaking from Arts & Crafts:**
- William Morris rejected machines (elitist, expensive)
- Bauhaus embraced machines (democratic, affordable)

**Application:**
- Design for industrial mass production
- Use modern materials (steel, glass, concrete, plywood)
- Standardization and modular systems
- Designer as engineer-artist

### 3. Geometric Abstraction
**Definition:** Pure forms (circle, square, triangle) are universal language.

**Influences:**
- Kandinsky's spiritual geometry
- Suprematism (Malevich)
- De Stijl (Mondrian)

**Application:**
- Typography based on circles and straight lines
- Furniture with geometric frames
- Architecture with cubic volumes
- Compositions with primary shapes

### 4. Primary Colors + Neutrals
**Definition:** Red, blue, yellow + black, white, gray.

**Theory:**
- Itten's color wheel
- Maximum impact with minimal palette
- Psychological effects of color
- Color as structural element

**Application:**
- Paintings and graphic design
- Furniture and product design
- Architecture (colored walls for spatial definition)

### 5. Asymmetric Balance
**Definition:** Dynamic equilibrium without symmetry.

**Purpose:**
- More visually interesting than symmetry
- Reflects modern, changing world
- Creates tension and movement

**Application:**
- Graphic design layouts
- Architectural facades
- Furniture arrangements
- Textile patterns

### 6. Grid and Modular Systems
**Definition:** Underlying mathematical structure organizes design.

**Purpose:**
- Rational, systematic approach
- Flexibility within order
- Scalability and standardization

**Application:**
- Typography grids
- Architectural planning (modulor)
- Furniture systems
- Graphic layouts

### 7. Truth to Materials
**Definition:** Materials should be used honestly, not disguised.

**Application:**
- Steel looks like steel (not painted wood)
- Glass transparency celebrated
- Concrete texture visible
- No fake finishes or veneers

### 8. Design for the Masses
**Definition:** Good design should be affordable and accessible to all.

**Social Mission:**
- Democratic design (not luxury goods)
- Improve everyday life through design
- Mass production lowers costs
- Function over status symbol

## Visual Language

### Typography
**Bauhaus Typographic Principles:**
- **Sans-serif only** (modern, functional)
- **Asymmetric layouts** (dynamic)
- **Geometric letterforms** (circles, straight lines)
- **Bold hierarchy** (size and weight contrasts)
- **Horizontal and vertical emphasis** (orthogonal compositions)
- **Lowercase preference** (Herbert Bayer's universal alphabet)
- **Photography integrated with type**

**Key Typefaces:**
- **Universal** (Herbert Bayer, 1925) - single alphabet, no capitals
- **Architype** (various Bauhaus experiments)
- **Joschmi** (Joost Schmidt)
- Later influence: Futura (Paul Renner, 1927)

### Color Palettes
**Primary Palette:**
- Red: #FF0000
- Blue: #0000FF
- Yellow: #FFFF00
- Black: #000000
- White: #FFFFFF

**Secondary/Extended:**
- Orange, green, purple (mixed from primaries)
- Grays (neutral tones)

**Usage:**
- High contrast compositions
- Color blocks define space
- Emotional/psychological effects
- Limited palette = strong impact

### Composition Principles
**Bauhaus Layout Characteristics:**
- **Asymmetric balance** (off-center, dynamic)
- **Diagonal lines** (movement, modernity)
- **White space** (breathing room, emphasis)
- **Geometric shapes** (circles, squares, triangles)
- **Overlapping elements** (depth, complexity)
- **Photomontage** (collage, juxtaposition)
- **Grid-based structure** (invisible order)

### Geometric Forms
**The Bauhaus Trinity:**
- **Square** = red (stable, earthbound, material)
- **Triangle** = yellow (dynamic, sharp, idea)
- **Circle** = blue (spiritual, cosmic, emotion)

**Kandinsky's Theory:**
Form and color have inherent psychological associations.

## Workshops & Disciplines

### Metal Workshop
**Directors:** László Moholy-Nagy, Marianne Brandt

**Output:**
- Lighting fixtures (table lamps, ceiling fixtures)
- Tea and coffee sets

### Weaving Workshop
**Directors:** Gunta Stölzl (most successful)

**Output:**
- Wall hangings and tapestries
- Upholstery fabrics

- Commercially successful
- Integration of art and industry

**Directors:** Marcel Breuer (most famous)

**Output:**
- Chairs (Wassily, Cesca cantilever)
- Tables and desks
- Storage units
- Modular systems

**Innovations:**
- **Tubular steel furniture** (lightweight, industrial)

**Iconic Works:**
- Wassily Chair (Breuer, 1925) - leather straps on steel frame

### Typography Workshop
**Directors:** Herbert Bayer, Joost Schmidt

**Output:**
- Bauhaus magazine design
- Exhibition posters and catalogs
- Advertising materials
- Signage systems

**Innovations:**
- Universal alphabet (no capitals)
- Photomontage in graphic design
- Geometric letterforms

- Asymmetric layouts
- Integration of photography and typography

**Legacy:**
- Foundation of modern graphic design

### Stage Workshop
**Directors:** Oskar Schlemmer, László Moholy-Nagy

**Output:**
- Triadic Ballet (Schlemmer, 1922)
- Light-space performances
- Costume and mask design
- Multimedia performance experiments

**Philosophy:**
- Human body as geometric form
- Space-light-movement integration
- Total artwork (Gesamtkunstwerk)
- Technology and performance

**Legacy:**
- Influenced modern dance and performance art
- Conceptual and experimental theater
- Multimedia art precedent

### Photography Workshop
**Not official workshop, but influential**

**Pioneers:**
- László Moholy-Nagy (photograms, camera-less photography)
- Herbert Bayer (photomontage)
- Walter Peterhans (taught photography 1929-1933)

**Techniques:**
- Photograms (objects on light-sensitive paper)
- Photomontage (collage with photographs)
- Experimental angles and perspectives
- Documentation of Bauhaus work

**Legacy:**
- Modern photography aesthetics
- Advertising photography
- Conceptual photography art

## Architecture & Spatial Design

### Bauhaus Building, Dessau (1926)
**Architect:** Walter Gropius

**Features:**
- **Glass curtain wall** (structure separate from facade)
- **Asymmetric composition** (different volumes)
- **Flat roof** (no ornament, functional)
- **Steel frame** (modern construction)
- **Workshop wing** (glass walls, transparent process)
- **Bridge connecting spaces** (admin offices over street)
- **Masters' houses** (faculty housing nearby)

**Significance:**
- **Manifesto in built form** - embodied all Bauhaus principles
- **International Style** prototype
- **UNESCO World Heritage Site** (1996)

### Masters' Houses, Dessau (1925-26)
**Architect:** Walter Gropius

**Design:**
- Semi-detached houses for faculty
- Cubic volumes
- Flat roofs
- Large windows
- White walls
- Integrated with nature

**Residents:**
- Kandinsky and Klee (shared double house)
- Moholy-Nagy and Feininger (shared double house)
- Muche and Schlemmer (shared double house)
- Gropius (single director's house)

### Dessau-Törten Housing Estate (1926-28)
**Architect:** Walter Gropius

**Purpose:**
- Affordable worker housing
- Mass production techniques
- Standardized building components

**Design:**
- Row houses (terraced)
- Modular construction
- Prefabricated elements
- Small gardens
- Community-oriented

**Significance:**
- Social housing prototype
- Bauhaus principles at urban scale
- Industrialized building methods

## The Bauhaus Diaspora (1933-Present)

### The Closure & Emigration

**Nazi Reasons for Closing Bauhaus:**
- "Cultural Bolshevism" (un-German, communist)
- "Degenerate art" (abstract, international, Jewish faculty)
- Modernism seen as threat to traditional German values
- Progressive politics (many left-wing faculty/students)

**Where They Went:**
1. **United States** (majority)
   - Gropius → Harvard (architecture)
   - Mies van der Rohe → IIT Chicago (architecture)
   - Moholy-Nagy → New Bauhaus Chicago (design school)
   - Albers → Black Mountain College, Yale (art)
   - Breuer → Harvard, private practice (architecture)
   - Bayer → New York, Aspen (graphic design)

2. **Israel** (then Palestine)
   - Arieh Sharon (White City, Tel Aviv - largest Bauhaus urban plan)
   - Shmuel Mestechkin
   - Munio Gitai Weinraub

3. **United Kingdom**
   - Gropius (1934-37, before USA)
   - Breuer (1935-37, before USA)
   - Moholy-Nagy briefly

4. **Switzerland**
   - Several faculty returned to Swiss design schools
   - Influenced Basel and Zurich design education
   - Contributed to Swiss Design development

5. **Soviet Union**
   - Hannes Meyer (1930-36)
   - Some students with communist sympathies

### Impact in the United States

#### New Bauhaus Chicago (1937)
**Founded by:** László Moholy-Nagy

**Evolution:**
- New Bauhaus (1937-38)
- School of Design (1939-44)
- Institute of Design (1944-49)
- Absorbed into Illinois Institute of Technology (1949-present)

**Legacy:**
- Spread Bauhaus pedagogy in America
- Influenced American industrial design
- Photography program particularly influential

#### Harvard Graduate School of Design
**Walter Gropius (1937-1952):**
- Chair of Architecture Department
- Trained generation of American modernist architects
- TAC (The Architects Collaborative) practice

**Students Influenced:**
- I.M. Pei
- Philip Johnson
- Paul Rudolph
- Ulrich Franzen

#### Black Mountain College (1933-1957)
**Josef and Anni Albers:**
- Founded progressive art school in North Carolina
- Bauhaus teaching methods
- Influenced American abstract expressionism

**Notable Students/Faculty:**
- Robert Rauschenberg
- Cy Twombly
- John Cage
- Merce Cunningham
- Buckminster Fuller

#### Illinois Institute of Technology
**Mies van der Rohe (1938-1958):**
- Director of Architecture
- Designed IIT campus (modernist masterplan)
- "Less is more" refined modernism
- Trained architects in International Style

### International Style Architecture

**Codified in 1932:**
- MoMA exhibition: "Modern Architecture: International Exhibition"
- Henry-Russell Hitchcock and Philip Johnson book: "The International Style"

**Characteristics:**
- Volume over mass (open plans)
- Regularity over symmetry
- No applied ornamentation
- Glass, steel, concrete
- Flat roofs
- Open floor plans

**Bauhaus Influence:**
- Gropius, Mies designs exemplified style
- Spread globally (NYC, Chicago, São Paulo, Tel Aviv)
- Corporate modernism (office towers worldwide)

## Influence on Modern Design

### Design Education Worldwide
**Bauhaus Pedagogy Became Standard:**
- Preliminary course (foundations)
- Learning by doing (hands-on)
- Integration of theory and practice
- Interdisciplinary approach
- Collaboration over competition
- Art school + technical training combined

**Schools Following Bauhaus Model:**
- Ulm School of Design, Germany (1953-68) - "New Bauhaus"
- Basel School of Design, Switzerland
- Black Mountain College, USA
- Cranbrook Academy, USA
- Royal College of Art, UK
- Parsons, Pratt, RISD, USA

### Industrial Design
**Bauhaus Principles Became Industry Standard:**
- Form follows function
- Mass production design
- Honest materials
- Minimal ornament
- Ergonomic considerations

**Influenced Companies:**
- **Braun** (Dieter Rams) - electronics
- **IKEA** - affordable, modular furniture
- **Herman Miller** - office furniture
- **Apple** (Jony Ive) - consumer electronics
